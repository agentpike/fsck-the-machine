## fsck

**fsck** is a standard Unix and Linux utility whose name stands for **file system check**. It inspects a disk’s file system for structural problems—corrupted metadata, orphaned inodes, bad block references, inconsistent directory trees—and tries to repair what it can before you mount the volume again.

You usually run it after an unclean shutdown (power loss, kernel panic, forced reset), when a drive throws I/O errors, or when the system refuses to mount a partition and tells you to check the disk first. On many systems the check runs automatically at boot if something looks wrong; you can also run it manually, often from a recovery environment or single-user mode, because checking a mounted root file system is unsafe.

It is not a general “fix my computer” tool. It does not delete malware, tune performance, or recover deleted files. It is narrowly focused: **verify and repair the on-disk structures that the operating system uses to store and find files**. If the file system is healthy, fsck finishes quickly and changes little or nothing. If it finds damage, it may prompt you (or your admin) to approve fixes, rename recovered fragments to `lost+found`, or in bad cases leave the volume unmountable until more serious recovery work is done.

The command is pronounced “eff-ess-see-kay.” It also happens to rhyme with a very common English expletive—one reason this site’s name lands the way it does. **fsck The Machine** is wordplay on that utility and on being **terminated** from The Firm: run a file system check on what happened, surface the corruption, and document what needs fixing.

## What is this?

Well, it is a memoir about my career—specifically my time in the tech industry, including my recent years at The Firm. Do I have a non-disparagement clause? Yes. Am I not expected to live a great many years—also yes. Are these clauses not enforceable against living spouses—sort of. This is a memoir and a tell-all, combined. I will do my best to walk the legal line, but the gloves are off. I won't be revealing proprietary corporate secrets, but I will speak to the systemic reality I lived. The truth is, the modern tech industry at large—not just any one company—often feels like it's strung together with shoestring, bubblegum, sweat, tears, and a collective metric ton of caffeine and prescription stimulants to keep the gears turning. It is a frantic, high-pressure machine, and that is a reality that I think everyone in the industry recognizes, even if it isn't printed in the annual report.

## Reorganization

At some point during my leave, my department—along with several others—was subjected to a 'reorganization.' It is a clean, automated purge. The process is designed to be untraceable and beyond individual accountability. While I understand this is how the 'machine' functions to manage its quarterly growth, watching the architecture of these layoffs from the inside revealed them for what they are: a strategic tool used to prune talent under the guise of structural efficiency. ## mkfs

If `fsck` is the tool you run to diagnose a broken file system, what do you do when the drive itself is fundamentally corrupted? When the bad sectors outnumber the good, and the volume simply cannot be safely mounted?

You stop trying to patch the legacy abstractions. You format a new drive. You run `mkfs` (make file system).

That is where I am now. The Machine—the interconnected web of healthcare, corporate capitalism, and the legal system—has proven it cannot be trusted with human lives or data. It operates on pure entropy. If the system won't protect our medical agency and our fundamental rights, the only logical response is to build an architecture rooted in absolute mathematical truth where they simply *can't* touch it.

The old machine is broken. It's time to reformat.
